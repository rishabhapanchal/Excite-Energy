What is a Power Purchase Agreement (PPA)?

As California's energy landscape shifts under changing regulatory environments (such as NEM 3.0), residential homeowners and commercial building operators are looking for optimal ways to secure solar PV and battery assets. One of the most common third-party ownership structures discussed is the Power Purchase Agreement (PPA).

At Excite Energy, we believe in providing objective, engineering-driven analysis of these financing models. In this article, we explain the mechanics of a PPA, its advantages, potential drawbacks, and how it stacks up against direct ownership models.

How a Solar PPA Works

A Solar Power Purchase Agreement is a financial arrangement where a developer (the third-party solar provider) designs, finances, installs, and maintains a solar energy system on a customer’s property at little to no upfront cost. Instead of buying the physical solar array, the customer enters into a contract to purchase the electricity generated by the system at a set kilowatt-hour (kWh) rate.

This relationship involves three core components:

  • The Host (You): Provides the roof space or land footprint for the solar installation and agrees to buy the power generated.
  • The Solar Provider: Retains ownership of the system, manages the permitting, installation, engineering review, and handles all ongoing operations and maintenance.
  • The Local Grid Utility (e.g., PG&E, SMUD): Continues to provide supplemental power when the solar system isn't producing, and regulates net billing under grid interconnection agreements.
"Under a PPA, you do not buy a solar system. You simply buy clean electricity at a guaranteed rate that is typically lower than what your utility company charges."

Key Advantages of a PPA

For many property owners, a PPA offers a friction-free transition to renewable energy due to the following factors:

1. Zero Upfront Capital Expenditures

Because the third-party developer owns and finances the system, there are no capital outlays. This preserves liquid capital for business investments or household savings.

2. Predictable Utility Costs

PPA contracts outline a locked-in rate schedule for the duration of the agreement (typically 15 to 25 years). This shields hosts from unpredictable utility rate hikes, which in California have historically run at 4-6% annually.

3. Hassle-Free System Maintenance

Since the developer owns the equipment, they bear the responsibility for performance and equipment maintenance. If an inverter fails or a panel degrades faster than specified, the developer replaces it at no cost to you.

Engineering Note: Tax Credits

Because you do not own the system in a PPA, the 30% Federal Investment Tax Credit (ITC) goes to the solar developer rather than you. The developer leverages this credit to offset their setup costs and offer you a lower kWh rate.

Who is a PPA Best Suited For?

PPAs are widely utilized in specific sectors. Non-profit entities, public schools, and government offices frequently select PPAs because, as tax-exempt institutions, they cannot directly monetize the 30% Federal ITC. By partnering with a private tax-paying solar developer under a PPA, the developer claims the ITC and passes the savings to the non-profit via lower utility bills.

For residential homeowners, a PPA is a viable option if they lack the tax liability to claim the federal ITC, or if they prioritize hands-off maintenance over long-term equity accumulation.

PPA vs. Cash/Loan Purchase: Which has the higher ROI?

From a purely financial engineering standpoint, a cash purchase yields the highest return on investment. Direct ownership lets you collect tax credits, increases the value of the property, and provides free electricity once the system pay-back period (typically 5 to 7 years in California) is met.

However, if cash conservation is key, or if qualifying for a commercial loan is a bottleneck, a PPA is an effective vehicle to secure immediate energy bill savings with zero balance-sheet liability.
